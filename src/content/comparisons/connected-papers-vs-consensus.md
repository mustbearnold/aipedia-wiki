# Connected Papers vs Consensus

[Connected Papers](/tools/connected-papers/) and [Consensus](/tools/consensus/) both sit in the research-discovery stack, but they should not be bought for the same job. Connected Papers is a visual graph tool: start with one known paper and map the nearby literature. Consensus is an academic Q&A and evidence-synthesis tool: ask a focused research question, inspect the cited studies, and use the Consensus Meter or Deep review workflow as a starting point.

## Quick Answer

Use **Connected Papers** when the first problem is "what papers belong in this neighborhood?" Use **Consensus** when the first problem is "what does the literature say about this claim?" Serious reviews often use both: map the field in Connected Papers, then test specific claims in Consensus and read the original papers before citing.

## Decision Snapshot

| | Connected Papers | Consensus |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary job** | Visual related-paper graph from one seed paper | Academic search, paper-backed answers, and study-agreement signals |
| **Best buyer** | Student, researcher, or analyst entering a new field | Student, clinician, journalist, or policy analyst checking a focused claim |
| **Current pricing signal** | Free limited graph tier plus paid annual academic/business plans; verify graph limits at checkout | Freemium Pro/Deep ladder; verify current message/review limits at checkout |
| **Best output** | Similarity graph, Prior Works, Derivative Works, paper list | Cited answer, study snapshots, Consensus Meter where applicable, Deep review |
| **Main risk** | It finds papers but does not evaluate or synthesize them | It can compress evidence too aggressively if the question is broad or contested |

## Where Connected Papers Wins

Connected Papers wins when discovery is visual and exploratory. Its product centers one clear move: provide a seed paper by title, DOI, URL, or arXiv ID and generate a graph of conceptually related papers. That helps users see foundational work, adjacent clusters, and newer derivative work faster than keyword search alone.

Choose Connected Papers when:

- you know one anchor paper and need the surrounding field
- the buyer is scoping a literature review, thesis, grant, or related-work section
- a visual map will reveal clusters and obvious missing citations
- you want to avoid AI synthesis until after the paper set is clearer
- the work is early-stage discovery, not final evidence grading

## Where Consensus Wins

Consensus wins when the user has a specific question and needs paper-backed orientation. Its official site frames the workflow around research sources, corpus search, Deep review, and filters. The strongest buyer fit is a focused claim where cited studies can be inspected, not a vague "tell me everything" prompt.

Choose Consensus when:

- the question can be answered from academic literature
- cited answer synthesis is more useful than a graph
- the buyer wants a fast read on study agreement before deeper review
- biomedical, clinical, health, psychology, or social-science evidence is central
- the output must become notes, citations, or a briefing after source inspection

## Key Differences

Connected Papers answers "what should I read next?" Consensus answers "what do these papers appear to say?" That difference matters more than pricing. Connected Papers does not replace reading, extraction, or citation-quality checks. Consensus does not replace search strategy, inclusion criteria, or expert interpretation.

The clean workflow is sequential. Start with Connected Papers when the field is unfamiliar. Move to Semantic Scholar or Elicit for broader search and extraction. Use Consensus for claim-level synthesis, then cite the original papers rather than the generated answer.

## Who Should Choose Connected Papers

Choose Connected Papers if you are building the paper set: students starting a thesis, analysts preparing a landscape scan, researchers looking for priors, or founders reading into a technical field. It is especially useful before the search terms are obvious.

## Who Should Choose Consensus

Choose Consensus if the buyer already has a research question and wants fast evidence orientation. It is a better first purchase than Connected Papers for "does X help Y?" or "is there evidence for Z?" questions, as long as the user still checks study design and source papers.

## Can You Use Both?

Yes. Use Connected Papers to find the literature neighborhood, then use Consensus to ask targeted questions about claims inside that neighborhood. For high-stakes work, add Elicit for structured extraction and Scite for citation-context checks.

## Bottom Line

Pick **Connected Papers** for visual discovery. Pick **Consensus** for cited research Q&A. If the buyer is lost in the literature, start with Connected Papers. If the buyer has a clear claim to test, start with Consensus.

## FAQ

**Which is better for a literature review?**
Connected Papers is better for early scoping. Consensus is better for claim-level orientation. A real literature review may need both plus source inspection.

**Which is safer to cite?**
Neither output should be cited directly. Cite the original papers. Connected Papers helps find them; Consensus helps triage what they appear to say.

**Which is better for medical or clinical questions?**
Consensus is usually more useful for focused biomedical questions because it returns cited answer synthesis. For high-stakes decisions, use it only as a starting point and review the underlying studies.
