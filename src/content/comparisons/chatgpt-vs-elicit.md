# ChatGPT vs Elicit

[ChatGPT](/tools/chatgpt/) and [Elicit](/tools/elicit/) both help with research, but they solve different parts of the job. ChatGPT is the broad academic assistant for brainstorming, methods explanation, coding help, file analysis, drafting, images, voice, and general web research. Elicit is the specialist for academic-paper search, systematic-review workflow, screening, extraction tables, alerts, and evidence reports.

## Quick Answer

Choose **ChatGPT** if you need one flexible assistant for mixed academic work: outlining, explaining methods, summarizing uploaded material, debugging analysis code, generating tables, drafting prose, and checking your own reasoning. Choose **Elicit** if the hard part is finding papers, screening papers, extracting structured evidence, or creating a repeatable literature-review workflow.

For most students and solo researchers, start with ChatGPT Free or Plus plus free academic search. For systematic reviews, scoping reviews, evidence maps, policy reviews, or clinical literature work, Elicit Pro is the more relevant paid upgrade.

## Where ChatGPT Wins

- **Breadth:** ChatGPT covers writing, study help, file analysis, spreadsheets, coding help, images, voice, apps, Codex, memory, and general assistant work in one account.
- **Drafting and explanation:** It is better for turning real notes into outlines, explaining methods, debugging Python or R, creating presentation structure, and stress-testing an argument.
- **General web research:** It is more useful when the question involves current products, policy, companies, documentation, or news rather than only academic papers.
- **Lower-friction first step:** Free and Plus are easier to justify when the user is not yet running a formal literature review.
- **Broader workflow fit:** ChatGPT can sit before and after the evidence workflow: research-question framing, analysis help, prose revision, slides, and publication-adjacent work.

## Where Elicit Wins

- **Literature-review workflow:** Elicit is built around finding papers, screening them, extracting fields, and generating research reports.
- **Evidence tables:** It is the stronger choice when the output needs sample sizes, populations, interventions, outcomes, effect sizes, limitations, or other custom extraction columns.
- **Systematic-review structure:** Elicit's help docs describe a guided workflow with protocol setup, search, title/abstract screening, optional full-text screening, extraction, and report generation.
- **Academic search surface:** The current pricing page lists unlimited search across more than 138 million papers, plus clinical-trial access on paid tiers.
- **Review-team features:** Scale and Enterprise add collaboration, admin controls, higher review/report capacity, paper figure interpretation, and enterprise controls.

## Pricing And Plan Guidance

**ChatGPT:** Start free if you only need occasional brainstorming, explanations, or study help. Upgrade to Plus when file work, image generation, memory, projects, deep research, advanced reasoning, and heavier usage matter. Pro is expensive and should only be considered when Codex, agent mode, or deep research is part of real weekly output.

**Elicit:** Basic is useful for exploration because it includes limited Research Agent access, two automated reports per month, paper search, summaries, chat with papers, source viewing, and Zotero import. Plus is the cheaper yearly-only upgrade for deeper research. Pro is the first serious systematic-review plan because it adds the dedicated workflow, 5,000-paper screening, 144 reports or systematic reviews per year, 20 extraction columns at a time, alerts, custom upload extraction, explanations, templates, and API access. Enterprise is the institutional route for custom deployments, SSO/SAML, 2FA, custom sources, and no training on your data by default.

Do not compare these plans as if they are interchangeable chatbot seats. ChatGPT Plus buys a broad assistant. Elicit Pro buys a literature-review workflow.

## Best Workflow

Use **Elicit first** when the source set does not exist yet. Define the research question, search papers, screen abstracts, extract fields, export or review the table, then inspect the original studies manually.

Use **ChatGPT after evidence is selected** to explain methods, identify weak claims, suggest structure, turn notes into an outline, debug analysis, or revise prose. Paste source excerpts or verified extraction tables instead of asking ChatGPT to invent citations.

For serious academic work, the safest stack is usually:

1. Semantic Scholar or database search for discovery backup.
2. Elicit for screening and extraction.
3. NotebookLM or a source packet for grounded note work.
4. ChatGPT or Claude for explanation, drafting, code, and revision.
5. Manual citation, methods, and quality assessment before submission.

## Who Should Choose ChatGPT

Choose ChatGPT if you are a student, analyst, writer, researcher, or operator who needs one flexible AI workspace. It is the better first purchase when the work includes writing, data analysis, code, charts, presentations, general web research, or mixed tasks outside academic papers.

Do not use ChatGPT as your only literature-review system if the work requires database coverage, inclusion criteria, extraction fields, audit trails, or defensible citation workflow.

## Who Should Choose Elicit

Choose Elicit if the bottleneck is formal evidence work: systematic reviews, scoping reviews, policy evidence maps, medical/public-health literature, psychology reviews, product research grounded in papers, or any workflow where a structured extraction table matters more than a polished answer.

Do not buy Elicit just because you want a smarter chatbot. It is a research-workflow product. If your real need is casual answers, summaries, or broad writing help, ChatGPT, Claude, NotebookLM, Perplexity, or Semantic Scholar may be cheaper or simpler.

## Trust Notes

Neither tool removes the need for human review. ChatGPT can sound confident without proving the source. Elicit can accelerate screening and extraction, but inclusion criteria, study quality, extracted fields, paywalled full text, and final claims still need expert inspection.

For academic, clinical, legal, policy, or regulated work, cite original sources, document search strategy, preserve inclusion/exclusion decisions, and disclose AI use if required by the institution, journal, funder, or employer.

## Bottom Line

ChatGPT is the better **general academic assistant**. Elicit is the better **literature-review machine**. If you only buy one and your work is mixed, start with ChatGPT. If the deliverable is an evidence table, systematic review, scoping review, or paper-screening workflow, start with Elicit.

## FAQ

**Can ChatGPT replace Elicit?**
Not for formal literature-review work. ChatGPT can help frame questions, explain papers, and draft prose, but Elicit is built for academic-paper search, screening, extraction, and review workflow.

**Can Elicit replace ChatGPT?**
No. Elicit is not a broad assistant for coding, image generation, general writing, presentations, voice, or mixed daily work.

**Which is cheaper?**
ChatGPT has a free tier and Plus at $20/mo. Elicit has a Basic free tier; current pricing surfaces Plus as a yearly-billed $7/user/month plan and Pro as $49/user/month monthly or $29/user/month billed yearly. The cheaper choice depends on whether you need a broad assistant or a systematic-review workflow.

**Which is safer for citations?**
Elicit is safer for paper-centered workflows because it is built around source viewing and extraction. But neither tool makes citations safe automatically. Open the original source before citing it.

**Should researchers use both?**
Often, yes. Elicit handles paper discovery, screening, and extraction. ChatGPT helps explain methods, revise prose, debug analysis, and package the work after the evidence is selected.
