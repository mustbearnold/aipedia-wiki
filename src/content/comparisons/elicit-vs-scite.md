# Elicit vs Scite

[Elicit](/tools/elicit/) and [Scite](/tools/scite/) both help researchers avoid weak evidence, but they enter the workflow at different moments. Elicit finds, screens, extracts, and reports on papers. Scite inspects how papers cite each other, using Smart Citations to show whether later work supports, mentions, or contrasts a claim.

## Quick Answer

Choose **Elicit** when the job is building a literature review or evidence table. Choose **Scite** when the job is checking citation context, validating claims, or grounding other AI tools in scientific literature through Smart Citations, MCP, API, or institutional access. Elicit structures the review. Scite stress-tests the citations.

## Mobile Decision Card

- **Best for paper screening:** Elicit
- **Best for claim/citation checking:** Scite
- **Best buyer:** Elicit for review teams; Scite for authors, reviewers, libraries, and R&D teams
- **Current paid floor:** Elicit Plus/Pro varies by billing cadence; Scite Basic is $20/month with a 7-day trial
- **Main watch-out:** Elicit extraction needs verification; Scite citation labels need human interpretation

## Where Elicit Wins

Elicit wins upstream. It is the stronger tool when a researcher needs to turn a research question into a paper set, screen the set, extract structured fields, and export a report or evidence table.

Choose Elicit when:

- the review starts from a broad question
- inclusion/exclusion criteria and paper screening matter
- the team needs extraction columns for study details
- reports, alerts, exports, and systematic-review workflow matter
- the buyer wants one flow from search to extraction

## Where Scite Wins

Scite wins when the question is "what happened after this paper or claim entered the literature?" Its pricing page lists Basic and Pro plans with Scite Assistant, full-text search, custom dashboards, citation reports, citation alerts, MCP credits, patents on Pro, organization access, API/MCP access, and developer credit-based options. Research Solutions' 2026 Scite MCP announcement also positions Scite as an evidence layer for ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, and other MCP-compatible tools.

Choose Scite when:

- a citation needs support/contrast context
- an author wants to avoid citing disputed or retracted work blindly
- a reviewer needs to inspect how a claim propagates through later papers
- an institution wants Smart Citations, dashboards, alerts, API, or MCP access
- an AI workflow needs scientific grounding beyond generic web search

## Key Differences

Elicit is about assembling evidence. Scite is about interrogating evidence. Elicit can help create the set of candidate papers; Scite can help decide whether the important papers and claims hold up in the citation network.

The strongest workflow uses both: build the candidate evidence base in Elicit, then run the central claims and heavily cited sources through Scite before writing. Neither tool removes the need to read the original papers.

## Who Should Choose Elicit

Choose Elicit for systematic reviews, scoping reviews, policy evidence synthesis, clinical literature review, grant support, and structured extraction from paper sets.

## Who Should Choose Scite

Choose Scite for citation validation, reference checking, claim credibility, literature context, institutional library workflows, and AI assistants that need access to scientific citation evidence.

## Bottom Line

Pick **Elicit** to build the review. Pick **Scite** to verify the citation context. If the job is extracting fields from papers, Elicit wins. If the job is knowing whether later literature supports or challenges a claim, Scite wins.

## FAQ

**Which should I use first?**
Use Elicit first for discovery and extraction. Use Scite when narrowing citations, validating claims, or checking whether a core paper is still reliable.

**Is Scite only a citation counter?**
No. The value is citation context and classification, not raw citation count. The current product also includes Assistant, dashboards, alerts, MCP, API, and organization paths.

**Can Elicit replace Scite?**
No. Elicit can summarize and extract from papers, but it is not built primarily around support/contrast citation networks.
