# arXiv moves toward one-year bans for unchecked AI-generated research submissions

arXiv is hardening its stance on papers that show obvious signs of unchecked large-language-model output. TechCrunch reported on **May 16, 2026** that Thomas Dietterich, chair of arXiv's computer science section, warned authors that submissions with clear evidence of unreviewed LLM generation can trigger a one-year ban and extra review requirements for future submissions.

Ars Technica reported the same enforcement posture on **May 15**, tying the warning to arXiv's existing author-responsibility rules.

## What changed

The rule is not an outright ban on AI writing assistance. The line is verification. Authors remain responsible for what they submit, including model-generated text, references, claims, and comments.

Examples that can trigger enforcement include hallucinated references, obvious model meta-comments, plagiarized text, biased content, incorrect results, or misleading claims that authors did not check before submission.

## Why this matters

AI research workflows now have a clearer quality bar. Tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Elicit, Consensus, and Semantic Scholar can help researchers summarize papers, draft explanations, and explore related work. But the output still needs source-level verification before it becomes a preprint.

This is especially important for literature reviews and survey papers, where fabricated citations can look plausible and can be difficult for casual readers to catch. A May 2026 arXiv preprint on non-existent citations found large-scale evidence that fabricated references are already appearing across scientific literature.

## Buyer take

Do not use an AI writing tool as the final authority for citations, mathematical claims, experiment summaries, or paper novelty. Treat AI-generated drafts as working material that must be checked against source PDFs, metadata, and reproducible evidence.

For universities and labs, the practical workflow should include citation validation, source-link checks, author review logs, and a final human responsibility gate before submission.

## What to watch next

Watch whether arXiv publishes a formal moderation update beyond the social-post clarification, how appeals work in practice, and whether other preprint servers or conferences mirror the same one-strike enforcement model.
